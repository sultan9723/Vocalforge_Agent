Furthermore, the development of these AI systems requires significant investment in both infrastructure and expertise, potentially creating a digital divide and excluding vulnerable populations from accessing this technology. The issue of algorithmic bias also needs careful consideration.  If the training data reflects existing societal biases, the AI system may perpetuate or even amplify these inequalities in access to nutritional resources and health outcomes. Addressing these biases through careful data curation and algorithm design is crucial for ensuring equitable access to the benefits of AI in nutrition planning. We also need to consider the impact on the role of human professionals, like dieticians and nutritionists. Will AI replace them, or will it augment their capabilities, allowing them to focus on more complex cases and patient interaction?  This transition requires careful consideration and strategic planning to avoid workforce displacement and ensure a smooth integration of AI into existing healthcare systems.
